The Problem With Chlorine (And It's Not Just Green Hair)

If you live in Florida and have access to a pool, you're probably swimming frequently. Maybe daily. And if you're reading this, you've probably noticed what that's doing to your hair: dry, brittle, straw-like texture, and if you're blonde, that lovely green tint.

Before You Swim: Prevention Strategies

Strategy #1: The Saturation Method

The science: Hair is like a sponge. If you saturate it with clean water FIRST, it can't absorb as much chlorinated water.

How to do it:

  1. Before entering pool, wet hair thoroughly with fresh water (shower or hose)
  2. Apply leave-in conditioner, oil, or dedicated swim protection product
  3. Work product through from roots to ends
  4. Optional: Put hair in braid or bun to minimize water exposure

Effectiveness: 60-70% reduction in chlorine absorption

Strategy #2: Protective Products

Certain products create a barrier between your hair and pool chemicals.

Best Options:

  • ‚Ä¢ Coconut oil or argan oil: Natural barrier, moisturizing
  • ‚Ä¢ Leave-in conditioner: Adds slip, reduces friction
  • ‚Ä¢ Specialized swim sprays: Formulated to repel chlorine
  • ‚Ä¢ Hair sunscreen products: Bonus UV protection

Application Tips:

  • ‚Ä¢ Use on WET hair (better absorption)
  • ‚Ä¢ Focus on ends (most porous, most damaged)
  • ‚Ä¢ Reapply after 2+ hours in water
  • ‚Ä¢ More product = better protection

If you swim laps every morning or have a pool you use daily, you NEED a swim cap and protective products. There's no way around it. Your hair can't withstand daily chlorine exposure without serious protection.

After You Swim: Damage Control

Step 1: Rinse IMMEDIATELY (Non-Negotiable)

The longer chlorine sits on your hair, the more damage it does. Don't wait until you get home.

  • ‚Ä¢ Rinse with fresh water within minutes of exiting pool
  • ‚Ä¢ Use warm (not hot) water to open cuticles and rinse out chlorine
  • ‚Ä¢ Spend at least 2-3 minutes rinsing thoroughly
  • ‚Ä¢ If no shower available, bring a spray bottle of fresh water

Step 2: Shampoo Properly

Regular shampoo doesn't fully remove chlorine. You need the right approach.

The Post-Swim Wash:

  1. Clarifying shampoo: Once a week to remove buildup (not daily‚Äîtoo stripping)
  2. Chelating shampoo: Specifically removes minerals and chlorine (1-2x/week for regular swimmers)
  3. Gentle sulfate-free shampoo: For other wash days
  4. Apple cider vinegar rinse: Natural option (1 part ACV to 4 parts water)

Step 3: Deep Condition

Chlorine strips moisture. You must replace it.

  • ‚Ä¢ After every swim: Regular conditioner (2-3 minutes)
  • ‚Ä¢ Weekly: Deep conditioning mask (10-15 minutes)
  • ‚Ä¢ Focus on ends: Most damaged area
  • ‚Ä¢ Consider: Protein treatments monthly for strength

Step 4: Avoid Additional Damage

Your hair is already stressed from chlorine. Don't add to it.

  • ‚Ä¢ Let air dry when possible (heat adds damage)
  • ‚Ä¢ Use wide-tooth comb (never brush wet hair)
  • ‚Ä¢ Apply leave-in conditioner before styling
  • ‚Ä¢ Minimize chemical treatments during heavy swim season

Green hair isn't caused by chlorine itself‚Äîit's copper and other metals in the water oxidizing on your hair (enhanced by chlorine). Blonde, gray, and light-colored hair shows it most.

Prevention:

  • ‚Ä¢ Wet hair with clean water before swimming
  • ‚Ä¢ Apply protective oil or conditioner
  • ‚Ä¢ Wear a swim cap if possible

Special Considerations for Florida

Florida swimmers face unique challenges: chlorine AND intense UV exposure. Both damage hair structure.

  • ‚Ä¢ Use products with UV filters

  • ‚Ä¢ Rinse more frequently if swimming during peak sun hours
